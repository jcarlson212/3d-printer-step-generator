# Modeling a Staunton knight

## Heritage
The Staunton knight (Jaques of London, 1849) is modeled on a horse's head from
the **Parthenon frieze** -- one of Selene's horses, now in the Elgin Marbles at
the British Museum. The reference is **classical sculpture**, not a cartoon horse:
a noble, slightly weary, powerfully arched head and neck.

## Anatomy / silhouette (read it in profile)
A knight reads almost entirely from its **side silhouette**. Capture, from base up:
1. **Circular collared base** -- a flat disc with one or two collar rings.
2. **Neck** -- rises and arches forward; the back of the neck (crest) is a strong
   convex curve; the front (throat) curves in then out to the jaw.
3. **Jaw & muzzle** -- a defined jawline; the muzzle projects forward and slightly
   down, ending in a blunt nose with flared nostrils. A slight mouth/lip line.
4. **Mane** -- a carved ridge running down the back of the crest, often stepped or
   grooved into locks.
5. **Ears** -- two alert ears at the top-back of the head, between mane and poll.
6. **Eyes / brow** -- a brow ridge and recessed eye on each side give it life.

## Proportions (standard Staunton)
- Height ~55-60 mm to the ears; base diameter ~30-33 mm.
- The head mass sits forward of the base centre -- balance it so the piece is
  stable (widen the base slightly rather than letting it get top-heavy).

## Style levers
- **Classical / marble**: smooth planar transitions, eroded/chipped edges, a
  fractured "fragment of antiquity" feel, matte surface.
- **Modern / minimal**: simplified planes, fewer mane locks, crisper geometry.

## Printability (PLA, FDM) -- design these in from the start
- The forward muzzle is a front overhang: keep its underside within ~45deg of
  vertical, or keep the projection short, so it doesn't need supports on the face.
- Ears and mane tips are fragile -- give them real thickness; no needle points.
- Reinforce the neck (load-bearing under a forward-heavy head); it is the #1
  failure point in shipping. Don't neck it down for looks.
- Blend deep undercuts under the jaw / behind the neck with fillets to cut support
  scars and stress risers.
- Prefer a near-monolithic body; if detail demands it, split into body + base /
  weighted insert that join after printing for durability and easier packing.
